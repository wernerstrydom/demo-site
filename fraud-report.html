The Cost of Fraud: A Decade of Losses and Legislative Accountability

A data-driven examination of fraud in the United States from 2015 to 2026, the legislative measures proposed to combat it, and how elected officials voted.

1. Executive Summary

Fraud against the United States government and its citizens has reached staggering proportions. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) estimates the federal government loses between $233 billion and $521 billion annually to fraud alone — not counting improper payments from administrative errors. In fiscal year 2025, federal agencies reported $186 billion in improper payments across 64 programs. Since 2003, total improper payments have exceeded $2.8 trillion.

Consumer fraud reported to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reached a record $12.5 billion in 2024, a 25% increase over 2023. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) recorded losses exceeding $16.6 billion in 2024, a 33% increase year-over-year. During the COVID-19 pandemic, an estimated $300+ billion was fraudulently obtained from relief programs.

Congress has passed a number of anti-fraud measures over this period, but voting records reveal a consistent and striking partisan divide: Republicans have voted overwhelmingly in favor of anti-fraud legislation, while Democrats have frequently voted against or abstained from such measures — particularly on bills targeting fraud in government benefit programs.

2. The Scale of Fraud: What It Costs Americans

Fraud against the United States takes many forms: improper payments from federal programs, consumer scams, healthcare billing fraud, pandemic relief theft, and identity fraud. The aggregate cost is measured in the hundreds of billions of dollars annually. 3. Federal Government Fraud & Improper Payments

The GAO's government-wide fraud estimate — between $233 billion and $521 billion annually — is based on data from fiscal years 2018–2022 and represents the most authoritative estimate available. This estimate covers fraud specifically, separate from administrative errors that also contribute to improper payments.

The largest contributors to federal improper payments are consistently the same programs:

The EITC Problem: The Earned Income Tax Credit has maintained an improper payment rate of 21–33% for over two decades. The IRS has acknowledged this persistently high error rate. Congress has repeatedly declined to fund robust EITC verification measures. In FY2024, approximately $21.9 billion in EITC payments were improper — representing the single highest error-rate program in the federal government.

5. COVID-19 Pandemic Fraud

The COVID-19 pandemic created the largest single fraud event in American history. With trillions of dollars disbursed rapidly through emergency programs, fraudsters — ranging from individual opportunists to organized criminal networks — exploited weak controls to steal an estimated $300 billion or more.

Criminal Convictions: As of December 2024, the Department of Justice had secured 2,532 criminal convictions related to pandemic relief fraud, with approximately 82% of defendants found guilty. Nearly half of all convicted defendants faced conspiracy charges, indicating organized fraud groups. Congress extended the statute of limitations from 5 to 10 years for PPP and EIDL fraud to allow continued prosecution.

6. Healthcare Fraud

Healthcare fraud — primarily against Medicare and Medicaid — represents the single largest category of federal fraud by dollar volume. The False Claims Act (FCA) is the primary civil enforcement tool, and FCA recoveries have reached record levels in recent years.

7. State-Level Fraud

Fraud at the state level mirrors federal patterns, with Medicaid, child care assistance, and unemployment insurance being the most commonly exploited programs. The response from state legislatures has varied significantly by party control.

Minnesota: A Case Study in Partisan Response

Minnesota became a national focal point for state-level fraud following the "Feeding Our Future" scandal, in which over $250 million in federal child nutrition funds were fraudulently obtained. The case involved organized fraud networks exploiting COVID-era relaxed oversight rules.

10. Analysis: Are Politicians Doing Their Jobs?

The data assembled in this report permits a factual assessment of whether elected officials at the federal level have acted to combat fraud against American taxpayers. The findings are unambiguous in several respects.

What the Voting Record Shows

On every recorded House roll call vote on anti-fraud legislation from 2022 to 2026, Republicans voted unanimously in favor — a combined 843 Yea votes and zero Nay votes across four bills. Democrats, by contrast, cast a combined 471 Nay votes against these same measures, with an average support rate of approximately 44%.

The pattern is consistent and intensifying. In 2022, the COVID-19 EIDL Fraud Statute of Limitations Act passed with near-unanimous bipartisan support (99% Democratic support). By 2024, Democratic support for the Consequences for Social Security Fraud Act had dropped to 26%. By March 2026, only 10% of House Democrats voted for the Deporting Fraudsters Act, with 186 Democrats voting against a bill that would deport non-citizens convicted of welfare fraud.

The Pattern of Inaction: The Consequences for Social Security Fraud Act (H.R. 6678) passed the House with 272 votes on January 31, 2024. Under the Democratic-controlled Senate of the 118th Congress, it was never brought to the floor for a vote. The bill would have made Social Security fraud and identity document fraud grounds for deportation of non-citizens. The Senate's failure to act meant this measure died without a Senate vote.

The Fraud-Enablement Question

The question of whether political inaction enables fraud is not merely rhetorical. Several specific mechanisms connect legislative choices to fraud outcomes:

Legislative Failure Consequence Estimated Cost
Failure to fund robust EITC verification (ongoing, 2015–2026) EITC maintains 21–33% improper payment rate; IRS lacks resources to verify claims ~$15–22B/year
Relaxed pandemic relief controls (2020–2021) Enabled $300B+ in COVID relief fraud; identity verification waived for speed $300B+ (one-time)

The Demographic Dimension

Several of the most contested anti-fraud bills specifically address fraud committed by non-citizens — including the Consequences for Social Security Fraud Act (H.R. 6678) and the Deporting Fraudsters Act (H.R. 1958). In both cases, Republicans voted unanimously in favor while the majority of Democrats voted against. Critics of these bills argue that existing law already provides deportation grounds for fraud convictions and that the bills are primarily symbolic. Supporters argue that the bills create explicit, mandatory consequences that current law does not reliably produce.

The EITC, which carries the highest improper payment rate of any federal program (33.5% in FY2024), has a well-documented pattern of fraudulent claims by individuals using false or stolen Social Security numbers. Multiple proposals to strengthen EITC verification have been introduced over the past decade; none have been enacted with meaningful enforcement funding.

The 2026 Executive Response

On March 16, 2026, President Trump signed an executive order establishing the Task Force to Eliminate Fraud, led by Vice President Vance. The order directs an interagency review of fraud in federal welfare programs including Medicaid, SNAP, EITC, and housing assistance. The White House fact sheet cited Minnesota's Medicaid fraud as a specific example, noting that "Feeding Our Future stole over $250 million" from child nutrition programs.

The executive order represents an acknowledgment that Congressional action alone has been insufficient to address fraud at scale. Whether the task force produces lasting structural reforms or serves primarily as a political messaging vehicle remains to be seen.

Conclusion

The data in this report supports several conclusions:

  1. Fraud is growing. Consumer fraud losses have increased more than 680% since 2015. Federal improper payments, while down from their COVID-era peak, remain at historically elevated levels ($186B in FY2025).
  2. Republicans have consistently supported anti-fraud legislation. On every recorded House roll call vote from 2022 to 2026, Republicans voted 100% in favor of anti-fraud measures.
  3. Democratic support for anti-fraud legislation has declined sharply. From near-unanimity in 2022 (99% support for EIDL fraud bill) to 10% support for the Deporting Fraudsters Act in 2026, Democratic opposition to anti-fraud measures has grown — particularly on bills targeting fraud by non-citizens.
  4. Senate inaction has blocked some anti-fraud measures. The Democratic-controlled Senate of the 118th Congress (2023–2024) did not bring the Consequences for Social Security Fraud Act to a floor vote despite it passing the House with 272 votes.
  5. The largest fraud vectors remain largely unaddressed. The EITC's 33% improper payment rate, Medicaid's $50B annual improper payments, and the ongoing pandemic fraud prosecutions all represent areas where stronger legislative action could reduce losses.

Whether the pattern of Democratic opposition to specific anti-fraud bills reflects principled policy disagreement, concern about civil liberties implications, or political calculation is a matter of interpretation. What is not a matter of interpretation is the voting record itself: it is documented, public, and consistent.
